⛰ The Three Great Fjords

Western Norway's fjord system is the most dramatic landscape in Europe. Three fjords define the Bergen region, each with a completely different character. Two are absolute must-dos.

The Crown Jewel

Nærøyfjord

Norway's narrowest navigable fjord. Sheer crystalline walls rise 1,400m straight from the water. National Geographic rated this the planet's #1 natural heritage site. This is the poster on every wall, the dream made real.

The Queen of the Fjords

Hardangerfjord

Norway's second-longest fjord (179 km) and fourth longest in the world. Where fjords meet orchards. Late June means cherry season is beginning, and Vøringsfossen is roaring with snowmelt. Softer, greener, pastoral.

The King of the Fjords

The longest (205 km) and deepest (1,308 m) fjord in Norway, second in the world only to Greenland. Nærøyfjord is actually a branch of Sognefjord. Impressively vast but its narrow arms are the stars.

The Smart Play

Do NOT visit Sognefjord as a separate trip. The Nærøyfjord cruise (via Norway in a Nutshell or independent booking) gives you the best of the Sognefjord system. Nærøyfjord IS a branch of Sognefjord -- you're already there. Spend your second fjord day on Hardangerfjord instead, which offers a completely different experience.

🚆 Flåmsbana Railway

One of the world's most scenic railway lines. 20 km. 20 tunnels. 863 meters of descent. One 180-degree turn inside a mountain. And a mythological dancer in a waterfall.

The Steepest Standard-Gauge Railway on Earth

From the high mountain plateau at Myrdal (867m) down to the fjord at Flåm (2m above sea level), this 55-minute journey packs more dramatic scenery per kilometer than almost any other rail experience in the world.

The Magical Stop

Kjosfossen Waterfall & the Huldra

The train halts for 5 minutes beside a 225-meter waterfall. During summer, dancers from the Norwegian Ballet School appear in the spray as the Huldra -- a seductive forest creature from Scandinavian folklore. She moves through water and rocks for 3 minutes, without a word, then vanishes. Many travelers call it the single most magical moment of their entire trip to Norway.

The performance happens on every scheduled summer departure (June 1 -- August 31), going both up and down. Be ready with your camera when the train slows -- it happens fast.

Take the Flåmsbana UP in the morning (07:20 or 08:20 departure). See the Huldra at Kjosfossen. Walk down to Kårdal. Cycle back to Flåm. This gives you the magical train ascent AND the physical adventure of the downhill ride. Arrive Flåm by lunchtime. Afternoon: Nærøyfjord cruise. Perfect day.

Do NOT Do This in One Day

The full loop in a single day is rushed and exhausting. Instead, spend a night in Flåm and break it into 2 days. Day 1: Bergen → Voss → Gudvangen → Flåm (cruise through Nærøyfjord). Day 2: Flåmsbana up + cycle back down, then afternoon Nærøyfjord cruise or kayaking. Alternatively, skip the package entirely and book each leg yourself for 20% savings and maximum flexibility.

Fjord surface10-14°C (50-57°F)

This water is COLD. Full thermal protection required. All reputable operators provide wetsuits, neoprene booties, spray skirts, and PFDs.

What Operators Provide

  • Stable tandem or single sea kayaks
  • Paddles, spray skirts, bilge pumps
  • PFDs / life jackets
  • Wetsuits (sleeveless "Long John" style)
  • Neoprene booties
  • Waterproof phone cases

What You Should Bring

  • Merino wool or synthetic base layer (NEVER cotton)
  • Fleece mid-layer for under/over wetsuit
  • Sunglasses with retaining strap
  • Sunscreen SPF 30+ (water reflects UV)
  • Complete change of dry clothes for after
  • Small waterproof dry bag for valuables

Early Morning = Magic (Ask for a Private Start)

Most operators depart at 9-10 AM. But the fjord is mirror-still before mid-morning wind picks up. Early morning light creates perfect reflections, almost no boat traffic, and you'll have the fjord to yourselves. Nordic Ventures offers flexible private options -- contact them about a 7:00 AM departure for the couple. This is a photographer's dream and absolutely worth the early wake-up.

🐟 Iris at The Salmon Eye

TEMPORARILY CLOSED -- JAN 2026

Iris at The Salmon Eye

Michelin-starred floating restaurant -- closed since January 2026. Monitor for possible relaunch.

Restaurant Iris received its first Michelin star in June 2024 and was named one of TIME Magazine's 100 Best Places in the world. Despite the accolades, operations were running at a loss. In January 2026, owner Sondre Eide (Eide Fjordbruk, a salmon farming family) announced the closure and laid off 13 employees. The family is pivoting to a venture called Watermoon, described as "commercially mature with significant global potential." Their statement: "For the Eide family, it is not 'enough' with Michelin. Our ambition is to ensure real global change so that the world's population eats more sustainable food from the sea."

What The Experience Was

  • World's largest floating art installation -- 1,000 sq m, 15m tall, covered in 9,250 stainless steel fish-scale plates
  • Floating in the Hardangerfjord near Rosendal, accessible only by electric boat
  • 5-6 hour expedition: boathouse welcome, underwater exhibition, 18-course tasting menu, rooftop open-flame course
  • Maximum 24 guests per day, 16 seats in the restaurant
  • Chef Anika Madsen (Danish). Menu 3,200-4,700 NOK per person
  • Washington Post: "18 courses, no murder: A floating restaurant right out of 'The Menu'"

What to Do Now

Monitor restaurantiris.no and thesalmoneye.com for any summer 2026 reopening announcement. The Salmon Eye visitor center IS still open (349 NOK adults, 2-hour guided aquaculture tour via electric boat from Rosendal). If Iris does not reopen, Bergen now has three Michelin-starred alternatives: Lysverket, Gaptrast, and Omakase by Sergey Pak (all newly starred 2025).

The Norwegian 3-Layer System

1

Inner Layer

Transport moisture AWAY from skin. Thin merino wool underwear or synthetic sports shirt. Never cotton -- it absorbs water and loses all insulating properties.

2

Mid Layer

Insulate and retain heat. Wool sweater, fleece, or lightweight down vest. This is your warmth engine for boat decks and morning kayaking.

3

Outer Layer

Block wind and rain. Waterproof, breathable shell jacket. Gore-Tex or similar. Bergen's rain is horizontal. A hood is essential, not optional.

Fjord Boat Tours

  • Waterproof hooded windbreaker (ESSENTIAL)
  • Merino wool sweater or fleece
  • Long-sleeve thermal base layer
  • Long pants (not jeans -- cold when wet)
  • Grippy walking shoes
  • Warm hat or beanie
  • Sunglasses + sunscreen SPF 30+
  • Compact umbrella + scarf

Kayaking (Extra Gear)

  • Moisture-wicking base layer (wool/synthetic)
  • Fleece mid-layer for under splash jacket
  • Complete change of dry clothes for after
  • Waterproof phone case or dry bag
  • Wetsuit provided by operator
  • Neoprene booties provided
  • NEVER wear cotton -- it kills warmth

You Do NOT Need

  • A heavy down parka
  • Oversized winter jackets
  • Ski gear of any kind
  • Thermal underwear (mid-layer is enough)
  • Bulky boots (comfortable walking shoes suffice)

Summer in western Norway is cool and damp, not cold. Think Seattle in April, not Minnesota in January.

The 19-Hour Daylight Advantage

For a couple who loves getting up early, this is extraordinary. Dawn light begins before 4:00 AM. The extended golden hour from 8:30-11:30 PM is magical for photography. It never gets truly dark -- just a soft civil twilight that merges one day into the next. You can hike at midnight. You will need a sleep mask.

📷 Photography Guide

At 60.4°N latitude in late June, the light behaves unlike anywhere else. A 3+ hour golden hour in the evening, another in the early morning, and no harsh midday shadows. This is a photographer's paradise.

Waterfall Tips

  • Fast shutter (1/500+): Freezes droplets. Use for the Huldra at Kjosfossen
  • Slow shutter (1/4-2s): Creates silky flow. Requires tripod
  • Polarizing filter: Reduces glare on wet rocks. Deepens sky
  • Spray protection: Carry a plastic bag for your camera body near falls
  • Lens fog: Moving from warm cabin to cold spray fogs lenses. Acclimatize first

Norwegian Photographer Wisdom

  • Plan around light, not locations. A mediocre spot in extraordinary light beats a famous spot in flat grey
  • Include people for scale. A kayaker in the fjord conveys the immensity
  • Shoot reflections early. Fjords are mirror-calm before mid-morning wind
  • Bergen weather changes fast. Rain/sun transitions create rainbow opportunities
  • Midnight silhouettes. Position subjects against the low late-evening sun

The Kjosfossen Challenge

The Huldra performance lasts ~3 minutes. The train stops, she appears in the spray, and everyone surges to the railing. Use burst mode, fast shutter speed, and be ready BEFORE the train comes to a full stop. No time for tripod setup. This is a grab-the-moment shot. Have your camera in hand, settings pre-configured, zoom at medium range.
